What is conveyancing?

The legal side of buying or selling a Solihull property – made clear

When you buy or sell a home, the change of ownership has to be handled properly in law. A solicitor or licensed conveyancer manages the whole process – carrying out title checks, raising searches with the local authority, handling mortgage funds, and registering the new owner with HM Land Registry. This is conveyancing, and for most straightforward transactions it takes somewhere between ten and fourteen weeks from the point your offer is accepted.

In Solihull, where property values are consistently among the highest in the West Midlands, getting the legal side right matters more than most people initially appreciate. A solicitor who is slow to respond or unclear on costs does not just cause frustration – in a competitive market where sellers can accept another offer, delays can cost you a property entirely. Hidden charges, vague estimates, and poor communication are the three complaints we hear most, and they are entirely avoidable.

Conveyancing in Solihull

What property buyers and sellers in Solihull need to know

Solihull occupies a particular position in the West Midlands property market. Its combination of excellent transport links – direct rail to Birmingham in under fifteen minutes, access to the M42 and M6, and proximity to Birmingham Airport – makes it one of the most consistently in-demand locations in the region. That demand brings its own complications when it comes to conveyancing, and understanding them in advance can make a meaningful difference to how smoothly your transaction runs.

High-value detached & semi-detached homes

Solihull's housing stock is dominated by detached and semi-detached family homes, particularly in areas such as Dorridge, Knowle, Bentley Heath, and Olton. These properties frequently sell at or above asking price in competitive conditions, and the stakes around getting the legal process right are correspondingly high. Any avoidable delay carries real financial risk.

School catchment purchases

A significant proportion of property transactions in Solihull are driven by school catchment areas – particularly for Tudor Grange Academy, Alderbrook, and the independent sector. Buyers moving specifically to secure a school place are often working to tight timescales and cannot afford lengthy delays. Solicitor speed and communication become critical in these cases.

New build activity near the NEC corridor

The Arden Cross development near Birmingham Airport and the NEC represents one of the most significant regeneration projects in the West Midlands. New build conveyancing in this area brings developer-set exchange deadlines, Help to Buy closures, and complex plot transfer documentation. Not all solicitors handle this type of transaction well – experience matters.

Leasehold flats in Shirley & town centre

Shirley and central Solihull have a concentration of leasehold flats, many within managed blocks. Leasehold transactions require additional legal work – reviewing the lease terms, obtaining management pack information from the freeholder, and checking service charge accounts and ground rent history. Slow managing agents are a common source of delay in this area.

Solihull also has pockets of older housing stock where title complications are more common – flying freeholds on some terraced properties, restrictive covenants on land that was historically part of larger estates, and Green Belt-adjacent plots where planning history needs careful review. In villages such as Knowle and Balsall Common, listed buildings and conservation area restrictions add a further layer of legal due diligence. None of these are insurmountable, but they do require a solicitor who reads the file carefully rather than processing it as a standard transaction.

Stamp Duty is also a more significant consideration in Solihull than in many neighbouring areas. With average property prices well above the regional mean, buyers frequently find themselves in higher SDLT bands. Your ConveyQuote quote will always include a Stamp Duty estimate based on the actual purchase price, so there are no surprises at completion.

Conveyancing costs are made up of the solicitor's legal fee and disbursements – third-party costs like search fees and Land Registry registration that the solicitor pays on your behalf. In Solihull, where property prices are higher than the regional average, some of these costs are proportionally larger. Here is what has the most impact on your total.

Factor What it means for your fees
Property price Land Registry fees scale with property value, and Solihull properties frequently fall into higher fee bands. Solicitors may also apply a modest scale to their legal fee on higher-value transactions due to the increased professional risk they carry.

Mortgage or cash If you are using a mortgage, your solicitor acts for both you and your lender – which adds to the workload. Cash buyers generally pay slightly less. Common questions

Frequently asked questions about conveyancing in Solihull

How long does conveyancing take in Solihull?

For a standard freehold purchase or sale in Solihull, most transactions complete within ten to fourteen weeks of an offer being accepted. Leasehold transactions – particularly those involving managed blocks in Shirley or the town centre – can take longer if the managing agent is slow to provide the required information. Chain transactions add further time at each link, and are common in Solihull's family home market where sellers are typically moving somewhere else. New build purchases near the NEC corridor may also be subject to developer timescales outside your solicitor's control. A reasonable expectation for most cases is twelve weeks, though your solicitor will give a more precise estimate once they have reviewed your file.

How much does conveyancing cost in Solihull?

Because Solihull property prices are above the regional average, some costs – particularly Land Registry fees and, in some cases, solicitor legal fees – run slightly higher than they would on a lower-value transaction. As a rough guide, a freehold purchase in Solihull might attract total conveyancing costs of between £1,400 and £2,200, depending on the price and complexity. Leasehold transactions typically cost more due to the additional legal work involved. Stamp Duty is a separate and significant cost for many Solihull buyers and will be clearly estimated in your quote. The most accurate way to understand your costs is to request a reviewed quote through ConveyQuote.

Do I need a solicitor based in Solihull?

No. In England and Wales, conveyancing is conducted almost entirely by email, post, and secure online systems. You will rarely – if ever – need to visit a solicitor's office in person. The solicitors on ConveyQuote's panel are regulated across England and Wales and are fully equipped to handle Solihull transactions without needing to be based locally. What actually matters is that your solicitor has experience with the type of transaction you are dealing with – whether that is a high-value freehold purchase, a leasehold flat, or a new build on a development site.

What can cause delays in a Solihull property transaction?

In Solihull, the most common sources of delay are: slow responses from managing agents on leasehold properties; long chains where a hold-up at one end affects everyone else in the sequence; mortgage lender processing times; and slow or incomplete responses to enquiries raised by your solicitor. In some parts of Solihull – particularly older village properties in Knowle or Balsall Common – title complications such as restrictive covenants or historical planning issues can also require additional time to resolve. Choosing a solicitor who chases proactively and keeps you updated throughout is the most effective way to reduce delays that are within your control.

What is the difference between a legal fee and a disbursement?

Your solicitor's legal fee is the charge for the professional work they carry out on your behalf. Disbursements are third-party costs your solicitor pays during the process and then recovers from you – things like the local authority search (raised with Solihull Metropolitan Borough Council), water and drainage searches, an environmental search, and the Land Registry registration fee. Neither should be a surprise, and on your ConveyQuote quote they are always shown as two separate line items so you can see exactly what you are paying and to whom.
