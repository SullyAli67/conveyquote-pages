What is conveyancing?

The legal work behind your property move – explained simply

When you buy or sell a property, ownership cannot simply change hands on a handshake. A solicitor or licensed conveyancer must handle the legal transfer – searching for issues with the land, reviewing contracts, managing mortgage funds, and registering the new ownership with HM Land Registry. This process is called conveyancing, and in most cases it takes somewhere between eight and sixteen weeks from the point an offer is accepted.

Choosing the right conveyancing solicitor in Birmingham matters more than many buyers and sellers realise. It is not just about the headline price. A firm that is slow to respond, unclear on costs, or overstretched can turn a straightforward purchase into weeks of chasing and uncertainty. In a city as active as Birmingham – with long chains, leasehold complications, and busy developers – the quality of your solicitor makes a real difference.

Conveyancing in Birmingham

What buyers and sellers in Birmingham need to know

Birmingham's property market has its own distinct character, and conveyancing here can involve complications that simply would not arise in a quieter or more uniform market. Whether you are buying a two-up two-down terrace in Erdington, a new-build apartment in Digbeth, or selling a semi-detached family home in Kings Heath or Harborne, the legal journey varies significantly depending on what you are buying, how it is held, and what the local searches uncover.

Leasehold flats

Birmingham's city centre and inner-city regeneration areas have a high concentration of leasehold apartments – particularly around the Jewellery Quarter, Broad Street, and the growing Digbeth and Southside districts. Leasehold adds complexity: management company accounts, service charge histories, ground rent structures, and lease length all need careful review before exchange.

New builds & regeneration

Post-Commonwealth Games development at Perry Barr, HS2-corridor schemes, and Eastside regeneration projects have brought significant new build activity to the city. New build conveyancing comes with tight developer exchange deadlines, shared ownership structures, and the need for solicitors experienced in dealing with national housebuilders.

Chain-heavy transactions

Birmingham's market moves at considerable volume, and chains of three, four, or five properties are common – particularly in Moseley, Stirchley, Sutton Coldfield, and Northfield. A delay anywhere in the chain affects everyone below it. A proactive solicitor who communicates regularly is not just preferable – it is essential.

Management company delays

Many Birmingham flats – from converted Victorian buildings to purpose-built developments – are managed by third-party companies. These organisations are often slow to respond to solicitors' enquiries, and it is one of the primary causes of delay in the city's flat transactions. An experienced firm will know how to manage this effectively.

Birmingham also has a substantial first-time buyer market, particularly in areas well-served by the metro and rail network. Shared ownership schemes, Right to Buy purchases, and buy-to-let acquisitions add further complexity and require solicitors with specific experience in those transaction types. Having a solicitor who understands Birmingham's local authority, Land Registry region, and common title issues makes a material difference to how your transaction progresses.

Understanding costs

What affects your conveyancing fees?

Conveyancing fees are made up of two parts: the solicitor's legal fee and disbursements. Disbursements are third-party costs – things like Land Registry fees, local authority searches, and drainage searches – that the solicitor pays on your behalf and passes on to you. Both should be clear from the outset. Here is what typically affects the total.

Factor What it means for your fees
Property price The higher the price, the higher the Land Registry fee. Solicitors may also scale their legal fee slightly on higher-value properties due to increased professional risk.
Leasehold vs freehold Common in Birmingham Leasehold transactions require significantly more legal work – reviewing the lease, raising enquiries with the management company or freeholder, and checking service charge accounts. Expect a higher fee for leasehold.
Mortgage or cash If you are buying with a mortgage, your solicitor also acts for your lender – which adds legal work. Cash buyers generally pay slightly less. Remortgages are priced separately and are typically more straightforward.
Gifted deposit If part of your deposit is a gift from a parent or relative, your solicitor must verify the source of funds for your lender. This normally carries a modest additional fee.
Shared ownership Common in Birmingham Buying a shared ownership property involves a more complex legal structure and a separate lease to review. Not all solicitors handle these – your ConveyQuote match will have appropriate experience.
New build Common in Birmingham Developer exchange deadlines, collateral warranties, and housebuilder contract requirements all add to the workload and the fee. This should be clearly disclosed up front – and it will be on your ConveyQuote quote.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions about conveyancing in Birmingham

How long does conveyancing take in Birmingham?

For a straightforward freehold purchase or sale, most Birmingham transactions complete within ten to fourteen weeks of an offer being accepted. Leasehold transactions – particularly those involving management companies – often take sixteen weeks or more, depending on how responsive the freeholder or managing agent is. Chain transactions add time at each link, and new build purchases can be subject to developer-driven timescales. A realistic expectation for most cases is twelve weeks, though your solicitor will advise once they have reviewed your specific circumstances.

How much does conveyancing cost in Birmingham?

Costs vary depending on transaction type and property value. As a rough guide, a standard freehold purchase in Birmingham might attract a total conveyancing cost – including searches, Land Registry fees, and legal fees – of between £1,200 and £2,000. Leasehold purchases typically run higher, from around £1,500 upwards, due to the additional legal work involved. New build and shared ownership transactions carry their own fee structures. We recommend requesting a reviewed quote through ConveyQuote to get an accurate figure for your specific situation.

Do I need a local Birmingham solicitor?

No – and this is a common misconception. In England and Wales, conveyancing is handled almost entirely by post, email, and secure digital portals. There is rarely any need to attend a physical office. The solicitors on ConveyQuote's panel are regulated across England and Wales and can handle your Birmingham property transaction efficiently regardless of where they are based. What matters is experience, communication, and the right expertise for your transaction type – not proximity to your postcode.

What causes delays in Birmingham property transactions?

The most common causes of delay in Birmingham are slow management company responses on leasehold properties, long chains where a problem at one end stalls everyone else, mortgage lender processing times, and slow responses to solicitors' enquiries from the other side. In Birmingham's new build market, delays can also arise from developers pushing back completion dates. Having a proactive solicitor who chases regularly and communicates clearly is the single biggest factor within your control when it comes to keeping things moving.

What is a disbursement, and why does it appear on my quote?

A disbursement is a third-party cost that your solicitor pays on your behalf during the conveyancing process, then recovers from you. Common disbursements include local authority searches (raised with Birmingham City Council), water and drainage searches, environmental searches, Land Registry registration fees, and any lender-specific costs if you are buying with a mortgage. These are payments made to external bodies, not solicitor profit. On a ConveyQuote quote, disbursements are always itemised separately so you can see exactly where every pound is going.
